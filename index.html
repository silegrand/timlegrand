About

Security, power,
and global governance

Professor Tim Legrand is an ARC Future Fellow and Professor in the Department of Politics and International Relations at Adelaide University. He is also Associate Dean (Research Performance) for the Faculty of Arts, Business, Law & Economics.

His research examines the national and international dimensions of global security decision-making, with a particular focus on transnational networks and institutions. His work spans global blacklisting and sanctions, digital security, terrorism, political violence, and political exclusion, drawing on public administration, law, sociology, and critical security studies.

He is Editor-in-Chief of the Australian Journal of International Affairs, Co-Convenor of the APSA Policy Studies Research Group, and adjunct Associate Professor of Public Policy at the Centre for Governance and Policy Analysis, University of Canberra.

He joined Adelaide in 2018, having held positions at the National Security College at ANU and the ARC Centre of Excellence in Policing and Security at Griffith University. He holds visiting fellowships from Sciences Po Paris, Johns Hopkins University, the University of East Anglia, and the University of Stockholm. His PhD in Political Science was awarded by the University of Birmingham in 2008.

Research

Projects &
Themes

Professor Legrand's research maps the architecture through which security decisions travel across states,, international organisations, and the informal networks of like-minded democracies.

Current Projects

ARC Future Fellowship
New approaches to combat the misuse of blacklists as tools of repression
2024–2028  ·  FT240100855  ·  $1,130,337 AUD

Defines the extent of malicious blacklisting used by authoritarian states and their alliances to justify persecution of dissenters and minorities. Using machine learning to decode hidden designation regimes, delivering the first comprehensive, publicly available, searchable dataset of global blacklists and policies to challenge malicious blacklisting.

National Intelligence Grant
Digital pathways to violent extremism in young Australians
Ongoing  ·  With Dr Nathan Manning & Dr Melissa-Ellen Dowling

Investigating the intelligence imperatives and policy implications of digital radicalisation pathways and the role of online environments in facilitating violent extremism among young Australians.

Past Projects

The Proscription of Terrorist Organisations in Illiberal States
2020–2022  ·  ARC Discovery Project DP200102447  ·  With Prof. Lee Jarvis, UEA

Examined how illiberal states deploy proscription law as a tool of political control, contrasting these modalities with liberal democratic practice.

Cyber Security in the Anglosphere
2020  ·  Cyber Security Cooperative Research Centre

Examined cyber-security policy collaboration and intelligence sharing within Anglosphere security networks.

Tackling Transnational Threats: The Architecture of Anglosphere Security Collaboration
2018  ·  Gerda Henkel Stiftung Foundation  ·  AZ 09/KF/18

Mapped the institutional architecture of security collaboration between Anglosphere states, examining the role of transgovernmental networks in managing transnational security threats.

Countering Foreign Interference
2021  ·  Dept. of Defence  ·  With Dr Melissa-Ellen Dowling

Developed frameworks for building democratic resilience against foreign interference in Australia's participatory processes.

Research Themes

Global Blacklisting & Sanctions

How authoritarian and democratic states use blacklists: designations that restrict movement, finances, and freedoms, and how these tools are weaponised for political repression.

Transgovernmentalism & Policy Transfer

The informal networks through which governments share, borrow, and adapt policy ideas across borders, especially within Anglosphere security communities.

Counter-Terrorism & Proscription

The legal, political, and symbolic dimensions of banning terrorist organisations, with emphasis on UK and comparative proscription law and consequences for democratic rights.

Digital Security & Liberal Democracy

The intersection of digital technologies, extremism, and democratic institutions, including foreign interference, online radicalisation, and the Anglosphere cyber-security posture.
