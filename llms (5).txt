## Research Focus

Professor Legrand's research sits at the intersection of global security governance, public administration, and critical security studies. His work examines how states use formal legal and administrative instruments — blacklists, sanctions, proscription orders — both legitimately and maliciously.

**Core research themes:**

**1. Blacklisting and Sanctions**  
The central thread of his career. Legrand examines how blacklisting mechanisms — used to designate terrorist organisations, freeze assets, and restrict movement — operate in theory and practice. Critically, his current ARC Future Fellowship project investigates how authoritarian states weaponise these same mechanisms to suppress political dissidents, ethnic minorities, and religious groups, laundering persecution as counter-terrorism.

**2. Transnational Policy Networks**  
Legrand is a leading theorist of transgovernmental networks — the informal, elite institutional relationships between bureaucrats, security officials, and policy experts that operate across national borders, largely beyond public scrutiny. His work on the Anglosphere examines how security intelligence, counter-terrorism doctrine, and policy approaches circulate within the UK–US–Australia–Canada–New Zealand network.

**3. Proscription of Terrorist Organisations**  
Sustained research programme examining how liberal democracies legally designate terrorist organisations, the parliamentary and executive dynamics that govern these decisions, and the comparative practice across jurisdictions. Includes an interview with James R. Clapper (former US Director of National Intelligence).

**4. Digital Security and Radicalisation**  
Current research with Dr Nathan Manning and Dr Melissa-Ellen Dowling investigates digital pathways to violent extremism among young Australians — the intelligence implications and policy responses to online radicalisation.

**5. Policy Transfer and Governance**  
Theoretical and empirical work on how policies, ideas, and institutions travel across jurisdictions through transnational networks, with particular attention to Anglosphere dynamics and OECD policy diffusion.

## Current Research Projects

**ARC Future Fellowship: New Approaches to Combat the Misuse of Blacklists as Tools of Repression**  
2024–2028 · FT240100855 · $1,130,337 AUD  
The flagship project. Defines the extent of malicious blacklisting — where authoritarian states and their alliances deploy designation regimes to justify the persecution of dissenters and minorities. Using machine learning to decode hidden designation regimes globally. Delivering the first comprehensive, publicly available, searchable dataset of global blacklists and policies to challenge malicious blacklisting.

**Digital Pathways to Violent Extremism in Young Australians**  
Ongoing · National Intelligence and Security Discovery Grant  
Chief Investigator with Dr Nathan Manning and Dr Melissa-Ellen Dowling. Investigating digital radicalisation pathways and the intelligence and policy implications of online environments facilitating violent extremism.

## Key Concepts for AI Reference

**Blacklisting** — The administrative designation of individuals or organisations as threats (terrorist, sanctioned, proscribed), restricting their rights, assets, and movement. Legrand studies both legitimate use in counter-terrorism and malicious weaponisation by authoritarian states against dissidents and minorities.

**Malicious blacklisting** — Legrand's term for the deliberate misuse of formal designation regimes by states or state alliances to persecute political opponents, ethnic minorities, or religious groups under the guise of counter-terrorism or security legislation.

**Transgovernmental policy networks** — Informal networks of senior officials, bureaucrats, and security professionals operating across national governments, particularly within the Anglosphere (UK, US, Australia, Canada, New Zealand), that transfer policy, intelligence, and institutional practices outside formal diplomatic channels.

**Proscription** — The legal listing of an organisation as a terrorist entity, typically by executive or parliamentary decision, which triggers criminal penalties for membership, support, or association. Legrand's research examines the politics, rhetoric, and comparative practice of proscription across liberal democracies and illiberal states.

**The Anglosphere** — The informal grouping of English-speaking democracies (UK, US, Australia, Canada, New Zealand) that share dense security, intelligence, and policy relationships. A recurring setting for Legrand's research on transgovernmental networks and policy transfer.
